Game Design Research

After reading Game Design Principles, here's what came to my mind. I play games too, mostly on my computer or phone, so I can understand their ideas. The ideas - clear goals, simple controls and rewards - are familiar to me. Take Super Mario, for example, you know you have to save the princess, and collecting coins keeps you wanting more. That's what they mean about goals and rewards. I've also rage-quit games that are confusing to operate, so the “game flow” bit is apt.

What struck me, though, is how practical it all sounds. For example, playtesting - trying a game over and over again to make sure it's fun - is a step I'd probably skip if I were making a game in-house. But I understand that I've also been quick to give up on games that felt boring. The sound design part also got me thinking. Once I accidentally muted the sound on a horror game, and the game stopped being scary as a result, proving that they're right when they say sound is important.

Overall, if you like games or want to make them, this is worth a read. It's not groundbreaking - most of it is common sense if you've played enough games - but it organizes the ideas in a coherent way. I wouldn't say this book changed my life, but for someone like me, who is more of a gamer than a creator, this book reminded me of what makes games tick.

The Power of Visual Thinking

The archived New York Times Learning Network blog post "10 Intriguing Photographs to Teach Close Reading and Visual Thinking Skills" (2015) presents a collection of thought-provoking images designed to help students develop critical thinking, observational, and analytical skills.

I find this resource very useful, especially for people who learn better by seeing things and those studying design, photography, or media. It encourages people to read images closely, which is an important part of knowing how to use visuals in fields like graphic design and branding. The selection of interesting photographs is a good way for students to learn to pay attention to what they see, which can be useful in many different subjects, like journalism and advertising.

My work in visual storytelling and branding (like my branding project NaChew in DES 116) helps me convey messages through design. Designing Effective and User-Friendly Modals

This article on best practices for modals, overlays, and dialog windows gives a detailed guide on how to design a modal window that is user-friendly and effective. It emphasizes the importance of using modals wisely to grab user attention, collect input, or display additional information without losing the overall context. It also highlights key components of a well-designed modal, such as providing an escape hatch, using descriptive titles, actionable button labels, appropriate sizing and location, and a lightbox effect to draw focus. It also stresses the importance of accessibility, ensuring keyboard accessibility and using ARIA tags to enhance usability for all users.

What stood out to me was the warning against using modals for error, success, or warning messages. It suggests that these should remain on the page to avoid interrupting user flow. The article also addresses the difficulties of using modals on mobile devices, and suggesting alternatives due to screen size and interaction issues.

Overall, the article provides valuable insights and useful advice for designing modals that improve user experience without causing frustration. It encourages designers to consider the user’s perspective and prioritize accessibility and usability in their designs.

Creating User Friendly Forms

Creating forms that are easy to use is essential to improving user experience and increasing conversions. In order to minimize cognitive load and simplify the process, this article highlights the significance of reducing input fields. While dividing lengthy forms into several steps can greatly increase completion rates, a single-column structure is advised for efficiency. Confusion is avoided by clearly identifying mandatory and optional fields, and starting with simple questions encourages user commitment.

Usability is improved by appropriately putting labels, ideally above input fields, particularly on mobile devices. It's best to utilize placeholder text carefully because it can occasionally work against you. User happiness also depends on being careful with default values and making sure input fields are the right size for the expected input.

The user experience can be further improved by making sure forms are tab-ready and provide suitable keyboards for various mobile input types. Form abandonment can be decreased by providing a "show password" option and outlining the necessity of sensitive information. Lastly, it's crucial to avoid reset buttons, give clear form feedback, and design action buttons that are both noticeable and informative.

By following these best practices, you can make forms that are not only simple to understand and fill out, but also have much higher conversion rates and make users happier overall.
